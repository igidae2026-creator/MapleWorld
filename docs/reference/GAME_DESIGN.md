# Game Design

This document is the canonical player-facing design target for MapleWorld.

The target is not generic MMORPG completeness. The target is a MapleLand-class early-to-mid progression loop that remains distinctive under MapleStory Worlds constraints.

## Core Player Promise

- Server-authoritative field hunting with route memory and visible competition.
- Fast readability in the first session without flattening the world into a single optimal path.
- Meaningful item replacement, potion pressure, and meso pressure from the first hour onward.
- Social value from shared hunting maps, party bonuses, boss access, guild growth, and trade surfaces.
- Stable live balancing without erasing MapleLand-style variance, friction, or memorable reward spikes.

## Progression Scope

The current canonical focus is the early and mid-game loop rather than full breadth expansion.

- Level bands must expose real bottlenecks and real release valves.
- Regions must preserve ladder identity rather than acting as anonymous EXP bands.
- Solo and party progression must both be valid, but they must not feel identical.
- First-job and early skill investment must create noticeable build direction.
- Equipment replacement must be frequent enough to feel rewarding and sparse enough to preserve chase value.

## Combat and Hunting

- Basic combat must feel legible immediately.
- Skill unlocks must add distinct tactical value, not only larger numbers.
- Map roles must remain separated into safe, alternative, and high-risk/high-reward loops.
- Rare spawns and minibosses must create interruptions that players remember.
- Boss encounters must act as progression gates and social anchors, not only payout buckets.

## Economy

- The economy stays single-world and server-authoritative.
- Monster meso generation, potion burn, repair pressure, upgrade cost, and tax pressure must stay in a controlled loop.
- Rare supply must be throttled rather than globally normalized.
- Market convenience must not destroy field value.
- Trade, auction, and shop surfaces exist to amplify progression choices, not bypass them.

## Social Play

- Shared fields are the default density anchor.
- Party bonuses must create real cooperation pressure in the right bands.
- Guilds must provide medium-term belonging and progression leverage.
- Bosses and event windows should naturally create congregation points.
- Channel routing should relieve congestion without dissolving social visibility.

## World and Event Texture

- Regional identity must stay visible through drop expectations, route difficulty, milestone rewards, and boss signatures.
- World events should alter pressure and incentives, not merely add flat multipliers.
- Canon-locked route memory and reward identity must be preserved.
- Early-loop beats must remain recognizable: first combat, first quest, first potion drain, first upgrade, first map transfer, first party opportunity, first miniboss.

## Anti-Goals

- Do not optimize the world into a single efficient treadmill.
- Do not remove friction that is carrying social density or reward anticipation.
- Do not broaden content coverage at the cost of first-session quality.
- Do not replace route identity with generic scaling tables.
- Do not treat proxy metric gains as success if player feel gates regress.

## Current Delivery Bias

Until the player-experience gates in `docs/reference/QUALITY_GATES.md` are green, prioritize:

- first-session clarity
- first-hour retention pressure
- day-one return motivation
- economy stability in the early/mid loop
- route variance and memorable rewards

Broader expansion comes after those gates hold under repeated validation.
